Study information

Help us to better understand if boosting overnight sleep by reducing obstructive sleep apnea (OSA) enhances brain function and performance.

OSA is a common sleep disorder and is linked with excessive and inappropriate daytime sleepiness, impaired thinking, and an increased risk for motor vehicle crashes and workplace accidents. However, not all patients report the same level of daytime impairment and it is still not completely known how OSA affects the brain

A key to understanding this is by investigating the brain activity of patients with OSA, and how this may change following continuous positive airway pressure (CPAP) therapy, the gold standard clinical treatment for OSA. This can be easily done through non-invasive EEG recordings from electrodes placed on the scalp that are a routine part of sleep studies. This study uses advanced high-density electroencephalography to investigate whether areas of ‘tired’ neurons that fall asleep in the awake brain underlie daytime impairments in OSA.

The purpose of this study is to examine the effect of using CPAP therapy, at two different settings, on daytime function and brain wave activity recorded in patients diagnosed with moderate to severe OSA. We will investigate brain activity during sleep and during awake daytime performance testing and compare the effect of using CPAP devices that deliver pressure in different ways.

This research is being carried out by researchers and doctors from the Woolcock Institute of Medical Research and the University of Sydney. The project is being funded by the Australian National Health and Medical Research Council Project Grant.

This study has been approved by Sydney Local Health District Ethics Review committee (RPAH Zone). Protocol Number X18-122.
